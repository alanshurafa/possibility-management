Discovering on Strikingly

I have an assumption: discovery is not a linear affair. So, Christopher Columbus did not set out to discover America.
But how does it work then? I read on this homepage that discovering can be an orthogonal path. I didn't know this word and looked up what it meant. What appealed to me is the explanation that orthogonal can mean non-redundant, non-overlapping, or even irrelevant. In other words, anything but linear.
Example: I was reading this home page about discovery, I didn't read through it from top to bottom, but stumbled on the word orthogonal. Looking up what that is, I stumbled on the word redundancy and something inside me caught fire.

I read a redundancy is the multiple mention of the same information. In a meeting, deadly boredom is guaranteed when that occurs: it's already been said, but not yet by everyone.

That is one meaning of redundancy. In medical technology, we speak of redundancy when a patient has two ventilators because he would suffocate should one fail. That's redundancy that can save his life.

I stumbled over the term orthogonal and left the linear path there. Later I turned off again: at the word redundant. I followed my own path, where my fires burned.

Here comes an idea for an experiment to explore orthogonality:
Start looking at the thing you want to discover. Observe yourself with a few percent of your attention, so you don't miss the little fires you start. Then follow the fire. Don't go straight ahead, linearly, but follow the fire.

In my case, the next fire on the path would be the word redundancy.

What is there to discover when everyone on a team says the same thing over and over for an entire evening, just in different words?

An exciting experiment for me. Where are your fires burning?

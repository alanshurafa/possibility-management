Stewardship on Strikingly

Nature does, of course, Steward itself, according to the laws of nature, from which viewpoint, human beings are expendable.

A human being can imagine that Nature's Stewardship is superior to the Stewardship of humans.

But Nature has a different timeline than humans. In Nature's timeline, humans hardly exist yet. We are a flash in the pan. If we go extinct, Nature does not care. Nature will keep rolling along even if Earth unleashes its carefully sequestered carbon and turns into a Venus-like planet with roiling clouds of 800 degree Celsius methane as an atmosphere and zero life forms.

Gaia might care if human beings go extinct. Human beings - the naked monkeys - are her special experiment. She evolved humans to have a nervous system that is complicated enough to become self-aware. This may have been her biggest mistake.

So far Gaia's Experiment has fucked itself over by eliminating Authentic Adulthood Initiatory Processes from its culture 6,000 years ago, and adopting hierarchical power structures that promote adolescent psychopaths to take them over and rapidly drive the future of all life on Earth into oblivion.

Humans look at a tropical forest and call it a jungle.

With Stewardship, humans would make that jungle into a garden, a park, with paths and benches. With Gremlin consumption, humans make the jungle into a monocropped desert, decimating the soils. 95% of a jungle's biomass is in the air, meaning up in the trees. The soil beneath the trees is sparse. When humans remove trees from the jungle they are removing almost the entire biomass. Then nothing else can grow there. Palm oil trees have a 40 year lifespan after which time the soil is poisoned and nothing else will grow there. Putting in genetically modified (GMO) corn or soybeans to feed cows, or putting in grazing cows to grind up into McDonald's hamburgers, destroys a billion year old habitat which cannot be repaired. Pumping out oil creates spills, leaks, and residues that poison rivers, and the new roads seem like walls to creatures who are cut off from their food collection routes in the jungle.

When a vine climbs up into a tree it can strangle the tree from light and water, eventually killing the tree. Nature does not care about this, but a human forest Steward might. The Steward might cut back the vine so both the tree and the vine can grow, providing diversity. Diversity leads to stability. When a species goes extinct, it diminishes stability in an ecosystem.

Drawing on data from more than 50,000 species, researchers have found that it requires the accumulation of genetic mutations over 2 million years for a new species to form. This holds true across vertebrates, arthropods (insects, arachnids and crustaceans) and plants. It takes a million generations to evolve lasting changes in a new species. It has also been discovered that on average, a species can adapt to evolving climatic conditions at a rate of only one degree Centigrade per million years.

Some Stewards who are aware of the rapid climate and temperature changes in the USA are, for example, making efforts to transplant trees, plants, and some animal species further north so they have some chance of relocating there. A friend of ours at an ecovillage in Germany illegally planted thousands of a species of pine tree in their already existing forest that can withstand more heat and drought than the local pine trees can stand. The local pine trees are already dying from the shortened winters, less water, and more heat. As the current forest dies off, the ecovillage's new trees will grow to replace them, but he will have to do the same thing again in ten years or so.

Human beings have no comprehension of 'a million years'. We can hardly wait a week to buy the latest model iPhone. Nature works in a different time frame and a different set of priorities than humans. Can you learn to implement Stewardship taking both these time and priority frames into account?

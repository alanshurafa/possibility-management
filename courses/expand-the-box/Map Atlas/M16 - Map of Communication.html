Map · Communication · Completion Loops · 12 Roadblocks

Map of Communication.

The architecture of an exchange between two people, the discipline of finishing what was started, and Thomas Gordon's catalog of the twelve things people reliably do instead of listening. The breakdowns are structural, not relational — they happen at named joins and can be repaired.

What this map shows

One toolkit, not three maps.

Any communication between two people runs a circuit with predictable structure and predictable failure points. Map of Communication names the circuit. Completion Loops name the discipline that closes the circuit so it does not leave residue. 12 Roadblocks name the twelve things people do instead of the circuit's actual work — twelve substitutions that look like listening, are not, and silently break the loop.

Together they account for why most arguments do not end — they pause until next time. The same fight in a marriage can run for a decade. The content varies; the structure is identical, because the underlying communications were never completed and the residue accumulates as energetic debt. The gremlin loves the unsaid.

Load-bearing claim: the breakdowns are structural, not relational. They happen at named joins, can be located, and can be repaired by a named practice. A reception gap is not a transmission failure; it is the listener running Listening 1 — which is why this map depends on the 4 Listenings and 4 Speakings upstream.

The PM-distinctive piece is step 5 of the completion loop — explicitly naming the conversation as complete, out loud, with words. Without that sentence, even a beautifully paraphrased exchange can leave residue. The explicit close is the load-bearing piece.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

Any communication runs a circuit. Speaker has an intention — something they actually want to convey. They encode the intention into speech. The listener receives the sound. The listener decodes the sound into meaning. The listener responds. The response runs the same circuit back. Five named joins.

At every join, a gap can open. The speaker has not located why — intention gap. The speaker is clear on the why but does not have words that carry it — encoding gap. The listener is running confirming-listening or already constructing their reply — reception gap. The listener heard the words but mapped them onto their own thoughtmap — decoding gap. The listener responds to a constructed version — response gap. Each gap compounds the next. After three turns, most "communications" are two parallel monologues. Both people feel unheard.

That account is structural. It is not about whether the people are good or bad communicators. Named failure points can be repaired by a named practice. The practice is the completion loop. Speaker speaks. Listener reflects back what the speaker wants received — in the listener's own words, until the speaker confirms it landed. Speaker confirms or corrects. Loop until the speaker says clean. Listener acknowledges — I got it. This is receipt, not agreement. Both name the conversation as complete, out loud. Are we complete on this? Yes. The formality is the price of precision. Pay it.

The reason completion loops matter is that uncompleted communications accumulate as energetic debt. The same fight runs for a decade — the content varies, the structure is identical, because the underlying communications were never closed. The residue is what people are living inside when they say the relationship is exhausting. You can run a completion loop retroactively — on a conversation from last week, last year, last decade. The opener: "Small thing from last month I want to circle back on, two minutes, nothing dramatic."

Then the 12 Roadblocks. Thomas Gordon catalogued twelve things people do instead of listening. Ordering. Threatening. Moralizing. Advising. Lecturing. Judging. Praising-to-control. Name-calling. Analyzing. Sympathizing. Interrogating. Distracting. All twelve look helpful. None of them are listening. These are not "bad" — most have a domain. Name the move. Drop the verdict. The roadblock you reach for first tells you which survival strategy your Box runs.

When a communication is stuck, the single most useful move is the meta-conversation. "I'm going to pause here. A conversation about the conversation." It pulls back from the content and makes the structure visible. One distinction to hold: the completion loop overlaps with reflective listening from NVC and Active Listening — both teach paraphrasing. What Possibility Management adds is the explicit naming of the conversation as complete. Are we complete on this? — out loud. Without it, even a beautifully paraphrased exchange can leave residue. The close is the work.

Embody it · solo practice

The small-stakes completion loop.

Solo prep for a real completion loop you will run with a person. Solo work first; the live loop happens in your actual life afterward. Sit. Notebook open. Center, ground, drop a bubble (Day 3 equipment).

Solo prep · 10–15 minutes · notebook · then a live loop in your life
  1. Identify one small unfinished communication. Spend 3 minutes scanning the last month. Pick one small unfinished item — not the biggest. I told my partner I would think about the vacation thing and never came back. Recent enough to be locatable. Write it in one sentence: what was left unsaid or unconfirmed.
  2. Locate the gap. Walk the circuit. Where did the original break happen?
    • Intention you were not clear why
    • Encoding you said it, but the words did not carry
    • Reception they were running confirming-listening
    • Decoding same words, different meanings
    • Response you responded to a constructed version
    Write the gap(s) you can locate.
  3. Name the roadblock you ran. What did you do instead of the work that would have closed the loop? Ordered, threatened, moralized, advised, lectured, judged, praised-to-control, name-called, analyzed, sympathized, interrogated, distracted? The one you reach for first is data about your Box. Write it.
  4. Draft the completion loop opener. Small, low-key, non-dramatic — it must NOT sound like the start of a breakup. "Small thing from [time] I want to circle back on. Two minutes. Nothing dramatic." Then the unfinished piece, said cleanly. Then: "Can you tell me what landed for you?" Out loud, say it once, in the room you are in.
  5. Name the close. Write the actual sentence that will close the loop. "Are we complete on this?" — yes, that one. Out loud, say it once. Notice — most people have almost never said this sentence. It will feel formal. That is the practice.
  6. Pick the time. Pick a specific 48-hour window in which you will run the loop. Write it in your notebook. Without the time on paper, the gremlin will keep the debt open indefinitely. Then close the notebook. Stand up. Three breaths.
The solo work is done. The loop itself happens in your actual life, on the time you wrote down. After running it, write three lines: what changed in your body when the close-sentence was actually said, what changed in the room, what changed in the other person — and what your gremlin did when an energetic debt suddenly closed.

Ground. Now.

  1. Stop. Stand up — both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact.
  2. Breathe out, longer than in — three slow exhales.
  3. Name three things you can see in this room. Out loud is best.
  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.
  5. One hand on your sternum, one on your belly. Notice your body is here.
  6. Then decide: continue, pause 10 minutes, or stop for today. Any of the three is fine.

Take as long as you need.

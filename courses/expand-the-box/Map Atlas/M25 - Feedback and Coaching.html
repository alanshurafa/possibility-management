New Map · Working With Others

Feedback and Coaching.

Two distinct moves people constantly blur: feedback reports the past — what worked, what did not — and coaching opens the future — possibilities, experiments, options to try. The whole map is one axis: past versus future. Everything else hangs off it.

What this map shows

One axis: past versus future.

The map splits a single page down the middle. Left half: Feedback. Right half: Coaching. Each half carries an Old Map (the distorted version the learner arrived with) and a New Map (the PM version), with the load-bearing sentence sitting in the center of each half.

Feedback's center reads: Feedback is about the PAST — what happened, what worked, what did not work. Coaching's center reads: Coaching is about the FUTURE — possibilities, experiments, "here's an option…", "what about…?" That single axis — past versus future — is the whole map. Everything else hangs off it.

The Old Maps show what each move degrades into when it is not sourced cleanly. Old-map feedback is distance, judgement, right/wrong, a power move, manipulation — they are under threat. Old-map coaching is advice, superiority, I know better, you should do this, hidden agenda. Both Old Maps are what most people are actually doing when they say the words "feedback" or "coaching."

The bottom of the map names the gate — when to use Feedback (there is agreement on goals · the person is asking · you care enough to be honest) and when to use Coaching (exploring options · creating new possibility · encouraging growth). The footer holds the whole thing together: Both come from CARE, CLARITY, and CONNECTION. Not from superiority. Not from threat.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

There are two different moves people constantly collapse into one, and the map's whole job is to pull them apart. The cut is time. Feedback is about the past — what already happened, what worked, what did not work. Coaching is about the future — what could be tried next, what possibility is available, what experiment the person might run. Past versus future. That is the axis. Hold it and the rest follows.

Start with feedback. PM feedback is not commentary on content — not the slide is busy or your argument has a gap; those are content notes, useful, but not what PM means by feedback. PM feedback is clean information about what worked and what did not. And here is the part most people miss: the same sentence can be three completely different things depending on where inside you it was sourced. If it comes from the witness — the part of you that just observed, without a verdict — it is feedback, and it lands clean. If it comes from the gremlin, the part that feeds on being right and being bigger, it is criticism, and it contracts the other person. If it comes from the rescuer, the part that cannot stand someone else's discomfort, it is advice, and it bypasses them. Same words on the page. Three different acts. The receiver feels which one it is in their body before they can explain why.

And feedback is gated. The map names three conditions, and they all have to be there: there is agreement on goals, the person is actually asking, and you care enough to be honest. Miss the gate — give your clean, accurate feedback to someone who did not ask — and it stops being honesty and becomes a power move. So inside a container where people agreed to be seen, feedback flows. Outside it, you ask first, and if the answer is no or unclear, you keep it.

Now coaching. Coaching faces the other way — toward the future. And the central PM move is this: coaching does not solve the client's problem. The job is to offer possibilities — here's an option, what about trying this, here's something to experiment with — and then hand the choice back. The moment you tell someone the answer, you have made yourself their authority and undone the work the map is for. A coach who is fixing is doing it wrong.

The two Old Maps are what these degrade into. Old-map feedback is distance, judgement, right/wrong, manipulation — the other treated as under threat, the room thick with anger and fear. Old-map coaching is advice from superiority — I know better, you should do this — hidden agenda, no real connection. Most people, most of the time, are running one of these two Old Maps while sincerely believing they are giving feedback or coaching.

What keeps both on the map is the footer: care, clarity, connection. Not superiority. Not threat. So the practical drill is simple. Before you open your mouth, ask: am I pointing at the past or the future? And: where in me is this coming from — witness, or gremlin, or rescuer? Two questions. They sort almost everything.

Embody it · solo practice

Write it both ways.

Solo. Notebook and pen. Center, ground, drop a bubble before you start. The map is not learned until you can feel, in a live conversation, which direction in time you are pointing — and from which part of you the words are coming.

Solo · 10–15 minutes · notebook required
  1. Pick one thing a person actually did recently. Spend two minutes. One specific action, by one specific person, in the last week — something you have an opinion about. Not a whole pattern. One thing. Write it in a single factual sentence: what they did, no verdict yet.
  2. Write it as clean Feedback (about the PAST). Two or three sentences, strictly behind the line of what already happened. What worked. What did not work. No should. No next time. No advice. If a sentence points at the future, it is not feedback — cross it out and move it to Step 3. Then check the source: read it back and feel which part of you wrote it. If it contracts you or sounds superior, the gremlin or rescuer grabbed the pen. Ground, and rewrite from the witness.
  3. Write it as clean Coaching (about the FUTURE). Now face forward. One possibility and one experiment, framed as the map frames them: here's an option…, what about…? Offer, do not prescribe. Notice the pull to write you should — and rewrite it as one thing you could try is. The test: would this hand the choice back to them, or take it away? If it takes the choice away, it is old-map coaching; soften it back into an offer.
  4. Read both aloud and notice. Say the Feedback paragraph, then the Coaching paragraph, out loud, in the room. Feel how different they are — one closes a past, one opens a future. Then the real data: which one did you reach for first, before you read this practice? Write one line. The one you default to is data about your Box.
    • Default A I default to feedback that is secretly advice.
    • Default B I default to coaching that is secretly judgement.
    • Default C I collapse both into one blurred move.
Close the notebook. Three breaths. Before you open your mouth in the next real conversation, ask two questions: am I pointing at the past or the future? And where in me is this coming from — witness, gremlin, or rescuer? Two questions. They sort almost everything.
Reflection

What landed?

One or two lines is plenty. Which did you reach for first — feedback or coaching? Could you feel the difference when you read them aloud? Which source — witness, gremlin, or rescuer — was holding the pen?

Ground. Now.

  1. Stop. Stand up — both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact.
  2. Breathe out, longer than in — three slow exhales.
  3. Name three things you can see in this room. Out loud is best.
  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.
  5. One hand on your sternum, one on your belly. Notice your body is here.
  6. Then decide: continue, pause 10 minutes, or stop for today. Any of the three is fine.

Take as long as you need.

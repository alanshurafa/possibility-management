Old Map · The Emotional Body

Old Map of Feelings.

The map of feelings you were issued as a child — by family, school, religion, peer group, workplace. It sorts the four feelings into good and bad, forbids three, dilutes the fourth, and runs on numbness. You are not here to fight it. You are here to see it.

What this map shows

It sorts feelings into good and bad — and that sort is the error.

The Old Map is the thoughtware most learners were issued as children — by family, school, religion, peer group, workplace, gender training. It sorts the four feelings into good and bad, forbids three of them outright, dilutes the fourth, and uses numbness to make the arrangement run smoothly.

The map is functional in patriarchal culture and dysfunctional in archiarchal culture. It does not malfunction in the learner — it does exactly what it was built to do: keep the learner controllable, predictable, and quiet about what they actually feel.

The cost is stored emotion, mixed-emotion soup, chronic low-grade numbness, and recurring drama — because feelings that cannot flow directly find indirect routes.

You are not here to fight the Old Map. You are here to see it precisely enough that it stops running you in the background.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

The map of feelings most of us arrived at this work with did not come from us. It was installed in childhood by the culture, the family, the schoolyard, the workplace. We did not choose it. We absorbed it. And it sorts feelings into two boxes: good ones and bad ones.

In the Old Map, three of the four feelings are bad. Anger is dangerous and out of control — especially if you are a woman, or trying to be a nice person. Sadness is weakness and self-pity — get over it, don't cry at work. Fear is cowardice and drama — hide it, push through, pretend confidence. And joy, the one that is allowed, is suspect — tone it down, don't make others jealous, who do you think you are.

Notice what is missing. There are no good feelings in the Old Map. There is a thin permitted band of muted positive states — "fine," "good," "not too bad" — and that's it. Three of the four are forbidden outright; the fourth is conditionally allowed and routinely diluted.

The Old Map is not malfunctioning. It is doing exactly what it was built to do. It keeps people functional inside cultures organized around control and dominance — patriarchal cultures. It cannot run a culture organized around bright principles, distributed authority, and conscious relating — what Possibility Management calls archiarchy. The Old Map is appropriate technology for the wrong destination.

The cost is high. Feelings the Old Map forbids do not disappear. They get stored as emotions, show up later at the wrong place, the wrong intensity, toward the wrong person. They drive drama, because feelings that cannot flow take indirect routes. And the whole arrangement is maintained by numbness — which is why most adults experience ten to thirty percent of what is actually present in their emotional body and call that their full range.

We are not here to fight the Old Map. We are here to see it. To name the specific rules we are carrying. Once the rule is named, it stops running us silently — and we get to choose what to do next.

Embody it · solo practice

The Old Map Roll Call.

Sit. Center, ground, drop a bubble. Notebook and pen. For each of the four feelings, in order — anger, sadness, fear, joy — name the specific rule you were issued.

Solo · ~10 minutes · notebook required
  1. For each feeling, write the answer to: "In the world I grew up in, what was the specific rule about this feeling?" Take them in order.
    • Anger what was the rule?
    • Sadness what was the rule?
    • Fear what was the rule?
    • Joy what was the rule?
  2. One sentence per feeling. Specific, not general. "In my family, fear meant you would be ridiculed, so I learned: never admit you are scared" — not "my family was tough on emotions." If two or three rules come, write them all.
  3. For each rule, name the source — parent, teacher, religion, peer group, profession, partner. The named source is half the work: it locates the rule in time and place, so it stops feeling like just-how-things-are.
  4. Read the four rules aloud, slowly, in your own voice. Notice your body. Which rule has the most charge? Which one did you almost not write down because it felt too small to count?
Put the page where you will see it during Days 5 and 6. This is your Old Map, named. It will be referenced when you do the Emotional Healing Process — because seeing the map is the start, not the end: the stored emotions it produced are still in your emotional body.

  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.

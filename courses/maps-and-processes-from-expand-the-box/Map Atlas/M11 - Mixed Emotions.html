When Feelings Don't Flow · The Emotional Body

Mixed Emotions.

What feelings become when they do not flow — stored, time-displaced, story-laden compounds of two or three feelings tangled with the past event that froze them. Not a pure feeling. Not just a stronger one. The path out is unmixing.

What this map shows

Part feeling, part memory, part story.

A mixed emotion is a stored state that is part feeling, part memory, part story. Two or three of the four feelings tangled together with the past event that froze them. It is not a pure feeling. It is not just a stronger emotion. It is specifically the mixed kind — feelings that were blocked at arrival, did not move, picked up a story, and stored.

The default condition of most adults, most of the time, is mixed-emotion soup — not clean feeling. Pure feelings are minutes long. Mixed emotions can last years. Decades, in the case of "stored" material the learner has been carrying since childhood.

This map names the common pairs and triads so the learner can recognize them — and names the path out, which is unmixing: identifying the component feelings one at a time, then letting each one move as itself through the Emotional Healing Process (M12). The pure feelings move. The mixed emotion dissolves. Energy returns.

This map sits between M10 and M12. M10 names what feelings are. M11 names what they become when they cannot flow. M12 is the practice that puts them back into motion.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

A pure feeling — anger, sadness, fear, joy — arrives, does its job, and passes in three to five minutes. That is what a feeling looks like when conditions allow it to move.

For most adults, most of the time, conditions do not allow it to move. The Old Map forbade three of the four. The Numbness Bar was installed so feelings would not register below a certain threshold. So a feeling arrives, gets blocked or swallowed or redirected or numbed — does not vanish — stops flowing, mixes with the memory of the event that triggered it, picks up a story, and stores.

The next time something resembling that event happens, what arrives is no longer a clean feeling. It is a mixed emotion. Part present-time stimulus, part 1997 dinner table, part story you have been telling yourself for twenty years. Part anger that was never allowed. Part sadness that was never received. Part fear that was never spoken.

You will recognize the common ones. Collapse and hopelessness — sadness plus fear. Resentment — anger plus sadness, because the anger could not land cleanly while the loss underneath was never grieved. Anxiety with a defensive edge — fear plus anger. Nostalgia and bittersweet — joy plus sadness, the most acceptable mixed emotion and the easiest to live inside forever. Depression is the classic — sadness on the surface, forbidden anger underneath, fear of your own anger underneath that. "Stress" is the most common in industrialized cultures — fear, anger, and sadness, all numbed.

Notice what is not on the list. Frustration is not a feeling. Overwhelm is not a feeling. Moodiness, burnout, "I'm just triggered" — none of those are feelings. They are stories about mixed emotions. The discipline of restating them — "I have some anger and some sadness mixed together right now" — is what makes the work possible.

The work is to unmix them. Name the components. Let each one move as itself. The pure feelings move. The mixed emotion dissolves. The energy that was being spent maintaining the storage returns to you. That is the Emotional Healing Process, and that is Day 6.

Embody it · solo practice

The Unmixing Restatement.

Sit. Center, ground, drop a bubble. Notebook nearby. Pick a small one — a recurring low-grade irritation, a mild "stress," a small "overwhelm," a flicker of nostalgia, a familiar resentment. Not a major decades-old wound.

Solo · ~10 minutes · notebook required
  1. Identify one mixed emotional state from the last 48 hours. Write it in the words you would normally use: "I was stressed about the meeting." "I felt frustrated when she said that." "I was overwhelmed by the inbox." One sentence, your usual vocabulary.
  2. Now restate it: "I have some [feeling] and some [feeling] mixed together about [trigger]." Use only the four.
    • Anger something I value is being violated
    • Sadness something I valued is gone
    • Fear something I value is at risk
    • Joy something I value is alive and present
  3. For each component feeling, ask: "Where in my body does this one live, and at what intensity is it present right now?" Write the body location and intensity (0–10).
  4. For each component, ask: "What is this one asking for, on the New Map of Feelings (M10)?" Anger asks for a boundary. Sadness asks for release. Fear asks for precision. Joy asks for sharing. Write the asked-for action for each.
  5. Read your unmixed version aloud. Notice how it lands in your body differently than the original "stress / frustration / overwhelm" wording. Precision opens space.
Stop there. Do not attempt to process this mixed emotion solo today. Unmixing the components is the practice. Moving them is the EHP (M12), and the larger pieces want a witness.
Reflection

What landed?

One or two lines is plenty. What did your "stress" or "frustration" turn out to be made of, when you named the components? How did the unmixed version land in your body, compared to the original wording?

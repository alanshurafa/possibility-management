Map · Drama & the Gremlin

Low Drama Triangle.

Three drama positions — Persecutor, Victim, Rescuer — that look like three different people but are one Box-pattern rotating through three faces. The triangle also names what the drama is for: gremlin food. Low drama is produced, not received.

What this map shows

Three positions, one machine.

Three positions a person rotates through whenever they are producing drama: Persecutor ("it's your fault"), Victim ("I have no choice"), Rescuer ("let me fix it for you"). These are not three personality types — they are three faces of one Box-pattern, and the same person flips between them inside a single conversation.

The triangle also names what the drama is for: gremlin food. Being right. Being wronged. Being special. Being needed. Being above it all. The drama is paying out a substance the gremlin wants. Naming the gremlin is not condemnation — you have a gremlin, you are not your gremlin — but the gremlin is the engine that keeps the triangle running.

Load-bearing claim: low drama is produced, not received. You are not in the triangle because someone put you there. You are in it because something in you eats what the position generates. "Getting pulled in" is itself a Victim framing — and a clue about your preferred food.

The move out is the Responsible Game: notice, name, step out, then ask three questions — what is actually happening, what do I actually want, what can I actually choose? A warning comes with it. At first the Responsible Game feels less alive, because the gremlin is starving and the body reads the absence of the charge as something missing. That passes. Staying with it is the work.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

The triangle has three positions. Persecutor at one corner — it's your fault. Attacks, blames, makes wrong. Victim at the bottom — I have no choice, this is happening to me. Collapsed, often controlling the room through misery. Rescuer at the third corner — let me fix it for you. Steps in unasked, takes over, builds dependency.

These are not three kinds of people. They are three positions one person rotates through, often inside a single conversation. The Rescuer who doesn't get appreciated becomes Persecutor. The Persecutor who gets challenged collapses into Victim. The Victim who finally gets angry weaponizes the helplessness. One machine. Three faces.

Why does anyone produce this? Because the gremlin eats what the positions produce. Being right. Being wronged. Being special. Being needed. Being above it all. Being beneath it all. The drama is gremlin food production. The gremlin is the part of you that wants the food. Naming the gremlin is not condemnation — you have a gremlin, you are not your gremlin. But the gremlin is the engine that keeps the triangle running.

The move out is called shifting to the Responsible Game. Four parts. First, notice you are in a position — the body usually knows first. Tight jaw is often Persecutor. Collapsed chest is often Victim. Pulled-forward toward someone else's problem is often Rescuer. Second, name the position, out loud or silently — I am in Persecutor right now. The naming itself is a small step out. Third, drop the posture — change your stance, breathe, ground. Fourth, ask three questions. What is actually happening, in fact, without my story? What do I actually want? What can I actually choose, from here?

A warning. The Responsible Game feels less alive at first. The drama was producing a charge and you mistook the charge for aliveness. Without the food, the gremlin is starving and your body reads the starvation as something missing. That passes. Stay with it. The gremlin is patient — when one food source dries up it finds another. Catching it is the work at week one, year five, year fifteen.

If you have met Karpman's drama triangle from transactional analysis, the shape is the same but the account is different. Karpman frames the roles as dysfunctional patterns. Possibility Management frames them as gremlin food production. The exit isn't "stop being dysfunctional." It's see the food, name it, and feed yourself differently.

Embody it · solo practice

The three-corner stand.

Find a room with enough floor for three spots about three steps apart, in a triangle. Mark them — coins, post-its. Label in your head: Persecutor at one corner, Victim at one corner, Rescuer at one corner. Bring to mind one small recent drama from the last seven days — not the biggest. The sigh, the eye-roll, the forty-five seconds of internal rehearsal, the conversation you walked away from with a story about what the other person really meant.

Solo · ~10 minutes · room to move · notebook
  1. Stand on Persecutor. Take the posture — chest out, jaw set, weight forward. Out loud, say what your inner Persecutor would say about this situation. One or two sentences. Feel the charge. Name the food — being right; being the one who sees clearly.
  2. Step to Victim. Take the posture — chest collapsed, shoulders down, weight back. Out loud, say what your inner Victim would say. I had no choice. Look what is happening to me. Feel the charge. Name the food — being wronged; being innocent; being special in suffering.
  3. Step to Rescuer. Take the posture — leaning forward, hands ready, eyes on the other person's problem. Out loud, say what your inner Rescuer would say. Let me handle it. Don't worry. Feel the charge. Name the food — being needed; being good; being above the problem.
  4. Now step off the triangle. Stand in the middle, equidistant from all three corners. Breathe. Drop the postures. Ground — feet on the floor, weight down, eyes soft. Out loud, ask the three Responsible Game questions:
    • Fact What is actually happening here, in fact, without my story?
    • Want What do I actually want?
    • Choose What can I actually choose, right now?
  5. Sit with each. Notice — the off-triangle spot is quieter, less charged. The gremlin will protest: this is boring, nothing is happening. That protest is the gremlin spotting the starvation. Notice it. Stay in the middle anyway.
Write two sentences about what you noticed when you stepped off the triangle. By Day 7 most learners discover they run all three positions, often in the same conversation — with one preferred entry point. The off-triangle stand is not a place you arrive at once; it is a move you make again and again, every time the body reports a position.
Reflection

What landed?

One or two lines is plenty. Which corner was your preferred entry point? What food were you eating there? What changed in your body when you stepped off the triangle into the middle?

Ground. Now.

  1. Stop. Stand up — both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact.
  2. Breathe out, longer than in — three slow exhales.
  3. Name three things you can see in this room. Out loud is best.
  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.
  5. One hand on your sternum, one on your belly. Notice your body is here.
  6. Then decide: continue, pause 10 minutes, or stop for today. Any of the three is fine.

Take as long as you need.

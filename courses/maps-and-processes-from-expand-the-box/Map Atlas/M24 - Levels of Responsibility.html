New Context · Radical Responsibility

Levels of Responsibility.

The same word — responsibility — means a different thing at each level, because each level is a different context. The level you speak from decides whether you are a victim, a rebel, a collaborator, or the author of what happens next. Same five syllables. Different universe.

What this map shows

The word does not soften. It changes meaning.

The map names three levels of responsibility and what each one produces. Child level produces blame, guilt, suffering. Adult level is your ability to create results. Radical level is responsibility is consciousness in action. The word does not soften or intensify as you move up the map — it changes meaning, because each level is a different context (context determines what the content means). Said from child level, "responsibility" means burden. Said from radical level, the same word means authorship. Same five syllables. Different universe.

This is the second map of Day 1 and it does the same job as the first: it works on the leftmost link of the chain. The course is not asking you to do responsibility better. It is asking you to notice which context you are saying the word inside, because that context is upstream of every option, choice and result that follows.

The map is not a ladder you climb once and graduate from. It is a set of moves available in any moment — you can speak from child, then adult, then radical inside a single conversation.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

There are levels to this word responsibility, and they are not degrees of the same thing — they are different contexts, so the word means something different at each one.

At child level, responsibility is something that happens to you. Someone else is in charge; you are on the receiving end. It's not fair. They did this to me. I have no choice. From here the word lands as weight, as fault, as blame — and what it produces, reliably, is suffering. A forty-five-year-old can speak from child level all day; it is not about age, it is about the context the word is being said inside.

There is a move out of child that is not yet adult: adolescent. You stop complying and start rebelling. I'll do the opposite. Watch me. It feels like power, but notice — you are still organized around the authority. You are defining yourself against the thing you say is responsible. The someone-else frame is intact; you have just flipped your sign.

At adult level the meaning changes. Responsibility becomes your ability to create results. I am responsible for me. I do my share. I keep my agreements. This is clean and it is necessary, and most of functioning adult life runs here. But it is still bounded by "my part."

At radical level the meaning changes again: responsibility is consciousness in action. I am at cause. I am the author of what happens here. This is not I caused everything and it is not it's my fault — that is blame, and blame is back at child level. Radical responsibility is being the one who chooses what this becomes next, knowing that inaction is also a choice with its own consequences. This is the context this whole course is sourced from.

And here is the part to hold: these are not four kinds of people. They are four moves, and you can make all four inside one conversation. The skill is noticing which one you are speaking from right now.

Embody it · solo practice

The four-level sentence.

This is the solo version of the Day 1 "Four Levels" interactive tool — same move, done in your body instead of on the screen. Stand. Both feet on the floor. Shoulders down. Three breaths, exhale longer than inhale. Pick one real sentence you actually said recently — to a partner, a boss, a kid, yourself — something with a charge on it. "You never listen." "Fine, I'll just do it myself." Write it down so it doesn't drift. You will say that one sentence four times — once from each level — and notice what your body does each time.

Solo · ~10 minutes · notebook required
  1. Child (90 sec). Speak the sentence as pure blame. Someone did this to you; you are on the receiving end; it isn't fair. Let the voice match. Notice your chest, your jaw, where the weight sits. This is the suffering posture. Return to standing neutral.
  2. Adolescent (90 sec). Same sentence, now as defiance. Watch me. I'll do the opposite. Feel the charge of rebelling against whoever is in charge. Notice it feels like power — and notice you are still aimed at someone. Return to neutral.
  3. Adult (90 sec). Same sentence, now from "my ability to create results." I am responsible for my part. I keep my agreements. The voice goes plainer, more level. Notice the weight come off the chest. Return to neutral.
  4. Radical (3 min). Same sentence one last time, from "I am at cause — I am the author of what this becomes." Not I caused it. The author of the next move. Notice what becomes available that was not available from child or adolescent. Notice the posture is the least dramatic of the four. Stay an extra minute in it.
  5. Close. Sit. Write three lines.
    • Line 1 Which level was my body's home stance on that sentence.
    • Line 2 Which level felt unfamiliar in my body.
    • Line 3 What became possible only from radical that was invisible from child.
Run this after you have watched 02 — New Responsibility & Culture. These are not four kinds of people. They are four moves, and you just made all four with one sentence. The skill is noticing which one is on the line — so that in the actual room of your actual life, you can catch the level you are speaking from and choose another.
Reflection

What landed?

One or two lines is plenty. Which level was your body's home stance? Which felt unfamiliar? What became possible only from radical that was invisible from child?

Ground. Now.

  1. Stop. Stand up — both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact.
  2. Breathe out, longer than in — three slow exhales.
  3. Name three things you can see in this room. Out loud is best.
  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.
  5. One hand on your sternum, one on your belly. Notice your body is here.
  6. Then decide: continue, pause 10 minutes, or stop for today. Any of the three is fine.

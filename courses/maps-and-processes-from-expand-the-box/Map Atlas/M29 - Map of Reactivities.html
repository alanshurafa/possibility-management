Reactivity · Six Kinds, Six Routes

Map of Reactivities.

Six different kinds of automatic reaction — Buttons, Voices, Activated Traumas, Hooks, Triggers, Imbalances — that all feel the same from inside but each route to a different tool, so the first move is always to name which one fired.

What this map shows

Not all reactivity is the same kind. Each kind has a different antidote.

A reactivity is when something external sets off an automatic internal reaction that is not a present-time conscious choice. Something happens, and you are already reacting before you decided to. Both the Box and the gremlin produce reactivity, and so does a tired body — which is exactly the problem the map solves.

The load-bearing claim: not all reactivity is the same kind, and each kind has a different antidote. From the inside they all feel identical — a jolt, a charge, a sudden no. So the untrained move is to reach for one favorite tool every time (process it, push through it, analyze it). The map says: stop, and ask which of the six is this? — because a Button needs Box work, an Activated Trauma needs the EHP, and an Imbalance just needs food, rest, or grounding.

Misdiagnosing the kind means applying the wrong tool, and the wrong tool does nothing. That is the whole function of this map: it is a routing table. Name the kind first; the name tells you the route.

Walk the six · and the diagnostic move

Take them one at a time.

Step through the six reactivity types — each with what it is and where it routes — then the diagnostic move the whole map turns on. Move at the pace of your body, not your reading speed.

Say it · the verbal map

Now explain it aloud.

This is the spoken version of the map. Read it through once, then close your eyes and say it in your own words. You teach the map by speaking it.

A reactivity is any time something outside you sets off a reaction inside you that you did not consciously choose. Something happens, and you are already reacting. The trap is that every reactivity feels the same from the inside — a charge, a jolt — so most people reach for the same response every time. This map says there are six different kinds, and each one has its own antidote.

Buttons are on the surface of your Box. A Box rule fires — this should not be like this — automatic and quick, the same reaction every time the button gets pressed. You handle a button with Box work: expand the Box. Voices are external authority figures you swallowed — the shoulds, the nags, a parent or a teacher still talking in your head as if the voice were yours. You handle a voice by recognizing it is not yours and declining its authority — we call that shooting the voices.

Activated traumas are the past happening in the present — something now reaches old stored material and it floods forward. The in-scope stored emotion routes to the Emotional Healing Process, with a hard line: actual trauma goes to a clinician, not the EHP. Hooks are gremlin seductions — the pull into a drama game, righteousness, sex-energy, becoming hookable so the gremlin gets fed. You catch the hook and decline the food. Triggers are specific trigger sentences or draining "vampire" energies — you close the cake, stop leaving yourself open to them.

And the sixth one is the one people forget: imbalances. Sometimes the reaction is not psychological at all. It is one of your five bodies out of balance — you are tired, hungry, or leaking energy. The antidote is not inner work. It is food, sleep, or grounding. So the whole move is: when you react, don't grab a tool — ask which of the six this is. A button, a voice, an activated trauma, a hook, a trigger, or just a body out of balance. Name it, and the name tells you the route. Often more than one is present at once — name the loudest.

Embody it · solo practice

Name which one fired.

Sit. Both feet on the floor. Center, ground, drop a bubble (Day 3 equipment — re-read M07 if it has fallen out of your body). Notebook open.

Solo · ~10 minutes · notebook required
  1. Bring one recent moment to mind — a time in the last few days you reacted. You snapped, withdrew, got hooked, got defensive, went cold. Not the biggest one. A small, ordinary one. Let it become present for about two minutes. Notice the charge that comes back with the memory. The charge is data, not a verdict.
  2. Now walk the six, out loud, one at a time. For each, ask the question and feel for the body's answer:
    • Button? A Box rule firing — this should not be like this — automatic, the same reaction you always have to that exact thing.
    • Voice? A should, a nag, an authority figure in your head — and is that voice actually yours, or did you swallow it from someone?
    • Activated Trauma? Did something old and much larger than the present moment flood forward, the past arriving in the present?
    • Hook? Gremlin food — righteousness, drama, being right, the pull to feed the loop. Did you get hooked because something wanted the food?
    • Trigger? A specific sentence or a specific person or energy that drained you the instant it landed.
    • Imbalance? Be honest — were you actually just tired, hungry, or ungrounded? Had you eaten? Had you slept?
  3. Name which one fired. Say it out loud: That was a [Button / Voice / Activated Trauma / Hook / Trigger / Imbalance]. Often more than one is present — name the loudest one first, then any others. Notice which naming makes your body do something — a flinch, a flush, a quiet oh. That somatic response is the right name landing.
  4. Name its route. Out loud: The route for this one is [Expand the Box / shoot the voices / EHP / decline the gremlin food / close the cake / bring my body to balance]. You are not doing the route right now. You are practicing the diagnosis — the single move the whole map is built around.
  5. Close. Put the notebook down. Stand up. Three breaths. Notice that naming the kind already changed the charge, even though you did nothing about it yet.
That shift is the map working: a reactivity you can accurately name is a reactivity you are no longer fully inside. And keep the carve-out — actual trauma processing is not in scope here; the EHP completes a stored emotion, and deeper trauma work belongs with a qualified clinician.

What landed?

One or two lines is plenty. Which of the six fired in your moment? What was its route? Did naming the kind change the charge before you did anything about it?

Ground. Now.

  1. Stop. Stand up — both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact.
  2. Breathe out, longer than in — three slow exhales.
  3. Name three things you can see in this room. Out loud is best.
  4. Name one thing you can hear. The fridge. Traffic. Your own breath.
  5. One hand on your sternum, one on your belly. Notice your body is here.
  6. Then decide: continue, pause 10 minutes, or stop for today. Any of the three is fine.

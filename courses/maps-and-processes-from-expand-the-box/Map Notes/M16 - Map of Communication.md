# M16 — Map of Communication · Completion Loops · 12 Roadblocks

*The architecture of an exchange between two people, the discipline of finishing what was started, and Thomas Gordon's catalog of the twelve things people reliably do instead of listening.*

![Map of Communication — Possibility Management map](../Maps/M16.png)

**What it is.** Any communication between two people runs a circuit with predictable structure and predictable failure points. **Map of Communication** names the circuit; **Completion Loops** name the discipline that closes it so it leaves no residue; **12 Roadblocks** name the twelve substitutions that look like listening, are not, and silently break the loop. Together they account for why most arguments do not end — they pause until next time, and the residue accumulates as energetic debt. Load-bearing claim — the breakdowns are structural, not relational: they happen at named joins, can be located, and can be repaired by a named practice.

**At a glance.** Circuit → intention, encode, receive, decode, respond (five joins, a gap at each) · Completion loop → speak, reflect back, confirm/correct, acknowledge receipt, name complete out loud · Energetic debt → incomplete exchanges accumulate as residue; retroactive loops recover it · 12 Roadblocks (Gordon) → ordering, threatening, moralizing, advising, lecturing, judging, praising-to-control, name-calling, analyzing, sympathizing, interrogating, distracting — none are listening, none are "bad" · Meta-conversation → the move when stuck. PM-distinctive piece → step 5, naming the conversation complete out loud.

## How to explain it verbally

This map bundles three things. The Map of Communication says any exchange runs a circuit, intention through encode, send, receive, decode, respond, with a gap at every join. Completion Loops are the discipline that closes the circuit so it leaves no residue: speak, reflect back, confirm or correct, acknowledge, and then the PM-distinctive move, name the conversation complete out loud. The 12 Roadblocks are Gordon's catalog of the twelve things people do instead of listening, each of which quietly breaks the loop. The reason most arguments never end is structural, not relational: they pause until next time, and the residue piles up as energetic debt.

**If you only remember one thing:** conversations break at named joins you can repair, and step five, naming it complete out loud, is what stops the residue.

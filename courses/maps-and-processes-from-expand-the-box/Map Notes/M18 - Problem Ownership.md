# M18 — Problem Ownership

*The first PM question when something is "wrong" between people — whose unmet need is actually in play — and the grammar test (I-statement vs you-statement) that makes the answer visible.*

![Map of Problem Ownership — Possibility Management map](../Maps/M18.png)

**What it is.** When something is "wrong" between you and another person, the first PM question is not *who is right* but: whose problem is this? Three honest options — my problem (I have the unmet need), the other person's problem (they do), or explicitly shared (both, by contract). The third is rarer than people think; most "shared problems" are one person's problem the other has been guilt-tripped into co-owning. Most adult conflict is misowning. The grammar test — I-statements own, you-statements misown — is the field-level diagnostic, and this is also the integration view of the Rescuer pattern from Day 7.

**At a glance.** Three options → my problem · their problem · explicitly shared (not two). Ownership → about whose unmet need is in play, not who is wrong. Owning ≠ fixing → it means being clear the need is yours and choosing what you do about it. Most "shared" → not shared without an explicit contract. No "we" in responsibility → "we have a problem" hides whose Adult is working. I-statements own; you-statements misown (functional, not stylistic). Rescuer pattern → one act of misowning followed by a Gremlin payoff.

## How to explain it verbally

Problem ownership starts with a different first question. When something is wrong between you and someone, do not ask who is right. Ask whose problem this is: whose unmet need is in play. Three honest answers: my problem, their problem, or explicitly shared by contract. That third is rarer than people think; most shared problems are one person's problem the other got guilt-tripped into co-owning. The grammar test makes it visible: I-statements own the need, you-statements misown it. Owning is not fixing; it means being clear the need is yours and choosing what you do. There is no *we* in responsibility: *we have a problem* hides whose Adult is working.

**If you only remember one thing:** ask whose unmet need this is before who is right, and watch I-statements own while you-statements misown.

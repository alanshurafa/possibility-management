# Experiment Bank

> A menu of between-module experiments. Each module gives you 1–3. This file expands the menu when you want more practice between modules — or when one of the module's experiments did not fit your week.

---

## What an experiment is

Per Day 4: an experiment is the operational unit of PM practice. Not a transformation. A rep. Reps compound.

An experiment has structure:

> **What I will do** — concrete, observable, in real life
> **By when** — specific deadline, usually within 48 hours
> **What I will notice** — what data you will collect about Box behavior, your body, the other person, the room

If any of the three is missing, it is not yet an experiment — it is a wish.

**How to pick one.** Read through the section for the module you most recently completed. Pick the experiment that produces the small discomfort of *I'd rather not, actually*. The Box's resistance is the signal you are at the right experiment. Comfortable experiments produce no new data.

**One at a time.** Run one experiment per module cycle (~3 days). Two parallel experiments compete for noticing bandwidth and you end up running neither cleanly.

**Capture in three sentences max.** *What I did. What happened. What the next experiment is.* Use your Beep! Book (the literal notebook). Capture within 10 minutes of running the experiment; details fade fast.

**Failed experiments are not failures.** They are Beeps. The experiment that did not produce the expected result *is* the data. The Shift comes from the noticing, not from the result matching the prediction.

**Lower-stakes variants.** Every experiment below carries a *Lower-stakes variant* line: a version that is internal-only, leaves no trace at home, or runs outside the house. If your intake flagged Screen 4, or if running the default version where you live could put your safety, housing, children, or income at risk, run the variant. This is not because you cannot handle the work; the course refuses to create risk in your actual life. The variant is the same rep. Items marked *“— (already private)”* are internal as written.

## Experiments around CONTEXT (Day 1–2 territory)

These work the leftmost link in the chain — the source-frame everything else flows from.

1. **The silent context shift in a meeting.** *What:* In the next meeting you walk into, silently choose the context *"I am at cause here"* before crossing the threshold. Hold it for the full meeting. Make no announcement. *By when:* The next meeting on your calendar. *Notice:* Where does your contribution come from differently? Where does the Box pull you back into spectator? What did you say or not say that you would not have without the held context?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

2. **Walking the chain backwards.** *What:* Pick one result this week you do not like. Walk the chain backwards out loud, on paper or to your partner, link by link — result → behavior → choice → option → thoughtmap → thoughtware → context. *By when:* Within 48 hours of catching the unwanted result. *Notice:* At which link did the chain originally bend? Most learners find it bent two links upstream of where they thought.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run the chain silently in your head on a walk or commute; if you write it, use a locked notes app or a page you discard.

3. **Name the Box once a day for a week.** *What:* Each evening, in your Beep! Book, write one sentence: *"Today my Box ran [the specific move]."* One specific move per day, for seven days. *By when:* Seven consecutive days. *Notice:* By Day 4, patterns repeat. Which moves keep showing up? Which situations trigger them? Which Boxes does your Box have?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Capture in a locked notes app, or use flat shorthand (*"B: left the room"*) that reads like a to-do list.

4. **Ask: from which context am I about to do this?** *What:* Before three discrete activities tomorrow — a meal, a conversation, a piece of work — pause for 15 seconds and ask out loud or silently: *"From which context am I about to do this?"* Name the context. Then do the activity. *By when:* Tomorrow. *Notice:* How often is the answer *"the context of getting through the day"*? What changes when you name a different context — *"the context of being curious about this person," "the context of making something I am proud of"* — before starting?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use the silent form throughout.

5. **The context-of-content audit.** *What:* For one decision you are mid-deliberating, write the same decision twice on paper: once under the heading "in the context of safety," once under "in the context of possibility." Write what each version chooses. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* The two decisions are likely different. Which context have you been deliberating from without naming?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Write the two headings in a locked notes app, or on one page you discard after reading.

6. **The content swap test.** *What:* Find a recent low-grade conflict in your week. Mentally substitute a different person — same content, different relationship — and notice if your interpretation of the content changes. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* If the content reads completely differently when the person changes, your conflict was about context (the relationship-frame), not the content.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

7. **The first-thing-in-the-morning context.** *What:* For three consecutive mornings, before checking the phone, sit upright and out loud name the context you are choosing to operate from today. One sentence. *By when:* Three mornings, before any screen. *Notice:* How often does the context default to *"surviving today"* if you do not actively choose? What changes in the day when you have chosen?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Name the context silently, or whisper it in the bathroom or the shower.

8. **The unwanted-result postmortem.** *What:* Pick one repeated unwanted result in your life — the kind that happens every Tuesday, every meeting with X, every Sunday evening. List the chain that produces it. Name the context at the top of the chain. *By when:* One sitting, this week. *Notice:* The context is almost never the one you would have named without doing the chain. The named-but-not-actual context is the one the Box has been narrating.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Do the sitting away from home (café, library, parked car) and keep or discard the page there.

## Experiments around the BOX (Day 2)

The Box is the survival-and-protection thoughtware you have been identified with. These experiments work the cut: *you have a Box. You are not your Box.*

9. **Catch yourself in one Box defense.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, catch yourself once in the act of defending the Box. The tell is usually a sudden tightness, a rehearsed sentence, or an internal "yes but…" *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* Whose voice does the defense use? What is it defending? What would happen if you did not defend?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

10. **Name a Box rule out loud.** *What:* Pick one Box rule you can name in plain language (*"I do not raise my voice," "I do not ask for help," "I finish what I start," "I do not look stupid"*). Tell your pairing partner the rule, when it was installed, and who enforces it. *By when:* Next partner exchange. *Notice:* Speaking the rule out loud changes its weight. Often the rule, once named, becomes optional — without you doing anything else.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Send the rule as a text instead of a voice message, or record the message outside the house (a walk, the car).

11. **Do one small thing your Box says you do not do.** *What:* Pick one thing your Box says *"you don't do that"* about. Smallest version possible. Eat a meal alone in a restaurant. Take a nap at 2pm. Wear something out of character. Ask a stranger a question. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* What did your Box predict would happen? What actually happened? The gap between prediction and reality is the size of the Box's exaggeration.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Pick an out-of-home version (the restaurant meal, the question to a stranger) rather than anything a household member would notice.

12. **The Box's morning sentence.** *What:* For three mornings, the first sentence the Box says when you wake — write it down before doing anything else. *By when:* Three consecutive mornings. *Notice:* The sentence is consistent. It is the Box's daily directive. It is not you.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Write the sentence in a locked notes app, or memorize it and write it down later, away from home.

13. **The Box-vs-Being test.** *What:* In one moment of decision today, ask: *"Is this my Box choosing, or is this me choosing?"* Do not yet act on the answer. Just ask, and note. *By when:* Today. *Notice:* The Box always has a louder voice. Being is quieter. If both showed up, which did you act on? Why?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

14. **One Box rule you decline.** *What:* Pick one Box rule you have named and decline it for 24 hours. Just one. *("Today I will ask for help on one thing." "Today I will not over-explain when I make a request." "Today I will be late once and not apologize.")* *By when:* One 24-hour window. *Notice:* What did the Box do when you declined? Did it escalate? Did it punish you afterwards with shame or doubt? That is the Box defending its territory.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Choose a rule whose decline is invisible at home: ask for help on one thing at work, or stop over-explaining in one email.

15. **The Box catalog.** *What:* In one 30-minute sitting, write a list of every Box rule you can name — fast, no editing, no analysis. Aim for 30 items. *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* Which rules surprised you by their specificity? Which rules contradict each other? *("I must be in control" and "I should never seem like I want control.")* The contradictions are the Box trying to manage incompatible directives.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Do the 30-minute sitting outside the house; keep the list in locked notes or a work drawer, or destroy it after reading.

16. **Naming the Box's costume.** *What:* When the Box runs today, name the costume it is wearing out loud (or in your head with witness). *"That's my Helpful Box." "That's my Doesn't-Need-Anything Box." "That's my Smart Box."* *By when:* One full day. *Notice:* By the end of the day, you will have catalogued four to seven costumes. The Box is not one thing — it is a wardrobe of rehearsed performances.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use the in-your-head form only.

## Experiments around LIQUID STATE + 5 BODIES (Day 3)

These work the energetic-body equipment and the body-by-body literacy that makes the rest of the course operational.

17. **Notice which body you spent the most time in today.** *What:* At the end of one day, list how much of the day each body was the primary location of your attention — intellectual, emotional, physical, energetic, archetypal. Rough percentages. *By when:* End of one day. *Notice:* For most office-working adults the intellectual body gets 80%+. The bodies that got little or no time are the ones the course is asking you to develop.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Keep the list in a locked notes app, or run it as a 60-second mental tally.

18. **Drop a grounding cord during a phone call.** *What:* In one phone call today, silently drop your grounding cord at the start of the call. Do nothing else differently. *By when:* Next phone call. *Notice:* What was different in the call? Did you interrupt less? Did you stay in the conversation when you would normally have started typing in another window? Did the other person speak differently to you?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

19. **Set a bubble before walking into a difficult environment.** *What:* Before entering one environment you find draining — a particular meeting, a particular relative's house, public transit at rush hour — pause at the threshold and set your bubble. *By when:* Next time you enter the environment. *Notice:* What was different at the exit? How much of "their stuff" did you bring out with you compared to before?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

20. **The five-body morning check.** *What:* For three mornings, in 90 seconds: how is my intellectual body today? Emotional body? Physical body? Energetic body? Archetypal body? One word each. *By when:* Three consecutive mornings. *Notice:* Which body is consistently ignored? Which has been overworked? What might today's day look like if it were paced to all five rather than just the loudest?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run all five questions silently; nothing needs to be spoken or written.

21. **The body-mismatch detection.** *What:* In one conversation today, notice if your bodies are saying different things — intellectual body agreeing, emotional body angry, physical body wanting to leave, energetic body collapsing. *By when:* Next medium-stakes conversation. *Notice:* When the bodies say different things, which one wins in your speech? Which one wins in your action? Which one was right?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

22. **The 90-second center recovery.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, the next time you notice you have gone off-center — caught the Box running, lost composure in a meeting, snapped at someone, become reactive — take 90 seconds within the next 5 minutes to find center, drop cord, set bubble. *By when:* Next off-center moment. *Notice:* Recovery time. How quickly does the equipment work when you actually run it under pressure?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

23. **The bubble-too-big or bubble-too-small audit.** *What:* Three times tomorrow — set phone reminders — ask: *"Right now, is my bubble too big, too small, or arm's length?"* Adjust. *By when:* Three reminders in one day. *Notice:* For most learners, bubble is too big when distracted (leaking into the room) and too small when intimidated (collapsed onto skin). Which way do you skew?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Label the phone reminders neutrally ("water," "stretch") so they read as nothing.

24. **The energetic-body field test.** *What:* In one ordinary moment — at your desk, on a walk — name what you are picking up energetically from the room or the people in it. *"Tension from the meeting I just left. Excitement from the team in the next room. Something stuck in my office that is not mine."* Do not interpret. Just note. *By when:* Three notings today. *Notice:* What you have been calling "your mood" may be 60% absorbed energy from your surroundings. The grounding cord is what discharges it.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

## Experiments around FEEDBACK + COACHING (Day 4)

These install the rapid-learning loop. Smaller cluster — the loop itself is the experiment-running engine.

25. **Ask one person for PM-grade feedback.** *What:* Ask one person already in transformational context with you (pairing partner, PM-aware friend, a coach) for PM-grade feedback on one specific thing. Script: *"I'm running an experiment. I want PM-grade feedback on one thing — [name it specifically]. What did you actually witness?"* Receive without rebuttal. Write what they said. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* What was the rebuttal you almost made? The rebuttal is the Box defending. Catching it is the win.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Ask by text, from outside the home, and choose someone outside your household.

26. **Give one person PM-grade feedback.** *What:* If they want it. Ask: *"Can I offer PM-grade feedback on one thing?"* If yes — give it. Specific, observable, no story. *("I noticed in the meeting this morning you said X three times. I am not naming this to fix you. I am offering a Beep.")* *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* How much pull is there toward making the feedback nicer than it actually is? PM-grade feedback declines the niceness pull.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Choose someone outside your household, and deliver it away from home or by message.

27. **Catch yourself slipping from witness into rescuer.** *What:* In the next partner exchange or a conversation with someone in pain, notice the moment you slip from witnessing into rescuing. *By when:* Next partner exchange. *Notice:* What was the impulse? Discomfort with their pain? Habitual helping reflex? Wanting to be useful? Once seen, the slip becomes a choice you make rather than a default that runs.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

28. **The Beep capture experiment.** *What:* For three days, each time something does not work the way you expected, write the Beep within ten minutes — *what you did, what happened, what the Beep was, what the Shift would be*. Do not yet act on the Shifts; capture. *By when:* Three consecutive days. *Notice:* By Day 3 you should have 6–20 Beeps. Patterns emerge. Some Beeps repeat. The repeating ones are pointing at the same Box rule from different angles.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Same ten-minute capture, but into a locked notes app in flat shorthand; sweep the lines into the Beep! Book only if the book travels safely with you.

29. **The one-sentence experiment.** *What:* Pick one conversation this week that would normally go a particular way. Plan one sentence — true, short, sourced from your body — that would shift the conversation off its track. Say it. *By when:* Next predictable conversation. *Notice:* What happened in your body before saying it? What happened in the other person? Most learners report the room rearranges around one sentence.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run the rep in an out-of-household conversation first (a colleague, a friend); the at-home version can wait for safer conditions.

30. **The feedback-decline check.** *What:* In the next week, notice every time someone offers you feedback and you decline it — by changing the subject, by explaining yourself, by counter-attacking, by going quiet. Count the declines. *By when:* One week. *Notice:* Which forms does your decline take? Each one is a Box defense. Cataloging them is the first move toward letting any feedback in.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

31. **The coaching-versus-fixing audit.** *What:* In one conversation where someone brings you a problem, notice whether you offer fixing (your answer) or PM coaching (possibilities the other person picks from). *By when:* Next such conversation. *Notice:* The pull toward fixing is enormous. PM coaching is *here are five possibilities, you choose* — radically different from *here is what to do*. Which were you doing?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

## Experiments around FEELINGS (Day 5)

These work the new map of feelings — the four energies as they actually are. Most learners find this section's experiments the most useful, because the reps lower the Numbness Bar.

32. **Name one feeling out loud each day for a week.** *What:* Each evening, out loud or to your partner: *"Today the feeling that was most present was [anger / sadness / fear / joy], at about [%] intensity, and the sensation was [location and quality]."* *By when:* Seven consecutive days. *Notice:* Which feeling shows up most? Which never showed up — likely meaning the Numbness Bar is still covering it?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Write the line in your Beep! Book or your Feelings Form row instead of speaking it, or say it alone in the car.

33. **Take the 5-minute test on a mixed state.** *What:* The next time you find yourself in a difficult internal state — irritation at a co-worker, anxiety about a deadline, sadness in your day — quietly start a stopwatch. Is this still happening after 5 minutes? After 15? *By when:* Next difficult state. *Notice:* If past 5 minutes, you are in an emotion. Which one of the four is underneath? You are not processing it — just clocking and naming.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

34. **Lower the Numbness Bar on one specific feeling.** *What:* Pick the feeling you most reliably do not register (often anger or joy). For three days, set three phone reminders per day. At each reminder, pause 30 seconds and ask: *"Is there 2% of [the feeling] in my emotional body right now?"* *By when:* Three days, nine reminders. *Notice:* By Day 3, the 2% becomes visible where it was not on Day 1. The Bar has moved one notch.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Label the reminders neutrally; the 30-second ask is silent.

35. **The low-intensity object test.** *What:* Pick three objects from your environment, one at a time. Hold each. Ask: *"What 2% feeling does this object bring up?"* Name it, locate the sensation, do not story. *By when:* One sitting, this week. *Notice:* Objects are loaded — old books, photographs, gifts from people, work tools — and the loadings are mostly invisible until asked. What is your house full of, energetically?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run it with pocket objects somewhere out of the house (a park bench, a parked car).

36. **One feeling, low intensity, three minutes.** *What:* Pick a feeling you have been carrying. In a private place, with consent from yourself, let it come up to **2–20%** for three minutes. Sound, movement, body. Then stop. Then ground. **Anything above 20% solo is unsafe** — that work belongs in a live, competent container (partner present, EHP-trained facilitator, or clinician). *By when:* One sitting this week, only if your pairing partner is reachable in the hour after. *Notice:* What 2–20% actually feels like in your body when you let it move instead of suppressing it.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Keep it to 2–10% with no sound and micro-movement only, or take the practice out of the house entirely (a parked car, a walk in an empty field) — partner-reachable condition unchanged.

37. **The Old Map roll call.** *What:* In one sitting, write the rule you grew up with for each of the four feelings. One sentence per feeling. Name the source — parent, school, religion, profession. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* The rules are precise once written. The precision is what lets them be set down. *("In my family, anger meant my father had been drinking, so the rule was: if you feel angry, leave the room.")*
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Write the four rules away from home, read them in a whisper, and photograph the page into locked storage if keeping the paper is not safe.

38. **The feeling-versus-thought-about-feeling distinction drill.** *What:* In the next 24 hours, three times: notice yourself "thinking about a feeling" rather than feeling it. Drop into the sensation. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* The mind goes to "I am angry because…" — the *because* is the story. The actual anger is in the bones. Can you find it without the story?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

39. **One clean feeling, said cleanly.** *What:* In one conversation in the next 48 hours, name one feeling cleanly, in the moment, without story or blame. *"Right now I feel some anger about what you just said."* No follow-up explanation. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* What did the other person do? What did you do next? Most learners discover that one clean feeling-statement changes the conversation more than 20 minutes of explanation does.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run it with someone outside the household, or inside your partner voice exchange, before any at-home version.

## Experiments around MIXED EMOTIONS + EHP (Day 6)

Smaller cluster — emotional healing process work is bigger and more bounded. These are the experiments around the edges of EHP, not full EHP runs (which require the proper container).

40. **Identify one stored emotion's components.** *What:* Pick one stored emotion you carry. Identify its components — which of the four feelings are in it, in what mix. *("My chronic resentment toward [person] is about 60% anger, 30% sadness, 10% fear.")* *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* Most stored emotions are mixes of three of the four. Once decomposed, each component can be worked separately. The mix is what made the emotion seem un-processable.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use initials or shorthand for any names; locked notes if paper is not safe at home.

41. **Identify a stored emotion and queue it for the right container.** *What:* For one stored emotion you carry, identify its components (see #40), locate where it lives, name what triggers it — and queue it for the right container. **Peer witness is not facilitator.** Your pairing partner can witness you naming the emotion and your queue plan; they do not hold the EHP. The EHP itself goes to: (a) a trained PM facilitator or Possibilitator, (b) a PM-aware coach, or (c) a trauma-qualified clinician if the material edges toward trauma. *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* The distinction between identifying-and-queueing (in scope for this course) versus running an EHP (requires a trained container). Mixing the two is the most common Day 6 mistake.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Send the naming and the queue plan to your partner as a message recorded away from home.

42. **Distinguish today's grief from old grief.** *What:* In one current loss — a person, a relationship, a phase of life, a chapter — notice the present-time sadness versus the layers of old sadness that are also activated. *By when:* One sitting, when the loss is alive. *Notice:* The present is often the trigger, not the source. How much of the current grief is for today, and how much is finally available to be felt because today made room? Both are valid; distinguishing them prevents misdirected action.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

43. **The body-storage location audit.** *What:* For three stored emotions you carry, locate where each lives in your body. Be specific. *("My grief about my mother lives in my throat and the upper chest. My resentment about my old job lives in my jaw and shoulders. My fear about money lives in the small of my back.")* *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* Bodies store emotions in geography. The geography is consistent across years. Locating gives you somewhere to bring breath, attention, EHP work.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use initials for people and places; locked notes if the page is not safe at home.

44. **The trigger-versus-source detection.** *What:* In the next emotional reaction you have that lasts past 5 minutes, ask: *"What is the trigger and what is the source?"* Trigger = today's content. Source = the older emotion the trigger activated. *By when:* Next stuck emotional state. *Notice:* The course's most expensive misunderstanding is treating triggers as sources. Once distinguished, the present-day situation can be addressed cleanly and the older emotion can be queued for the right container.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

45. **The completed-versus-uncompleted inventory.** *What:* List five emotional events in your life — losses, ruptures, betrayals, joys, transitions — and mark which are completed (have arrived, peaked, dissipated) and which are uncompleted (still loop, still trigger, still occupy bandwidth). *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* The uncompleted ones are the candidates for EHP work, in proper containers, in the right order. The list is the map.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Keep the list in locked notes, or write each event as a single neutral word only you can decode.

## Experiments around LOW DRAMA (Day 7)

These work the daily drama you have been generating and the gremlin food it produces.

46. **Catch one drama position in 48 hours.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, catch yourself once in Persecutor, Victim, or Rescuer. Name which. Do not stop performing it; just see it. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* Most learners catch their default position first (it is the easiest). The other two are harder to see because they show up under different conditions. Which is your default?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

47. **Name one of your gremlin foods out loud.** *What:* Pick one gremlin food you can admit to (being right, being misunderstood, being a victim, being a savior, being uniquely-burdened, being above-it-all). Tell your pairing partner: *"One of my gremlin foods is [X]."* *By when:* Next partner exchange. *Notice:* The discomfort of naming. The gremlin food works in the dark. Naming dims its energy. Often by 50% in the moment of speaking.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Send it as a text from outside the home instead of a message recorded at the kitchen table.

48. **Decline drama once and notice what's left.** *What:* In one situation in the next 48 hours where drama would normally happen — a habitual complaint conversation, a re-running of the same fight, a familiar gossip exchange — decline. Step out. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* What is left when the drama is declined? Often: silence, awkwardness, a feeling of emptiness, a glimpse of what the drama was substituting for. The emptiness is data, not a problem.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Decline an out-of-home drama first: a workplace gossip thread, a group-chat complaint loop.

49. **Catch the fighting-reality move.** *What:* Notice the next time you think, out loud or silently: *"this should not be happening."* That is the first move of every low drama. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* How many times a day does this thought arise? For most learners, dozens. Each one initiates a drama. Catching the thought is catching the drama at the source.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

50. **The drama-food accounting.** *What:* List your top five drama positions in the last 7 days. For each, name the gremlin food it produced. *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* The same gremlin food often produces three different drama positions in three different relationships. The drama varies; the food is consistent.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Locked notes; positions and foods in shorthand.

51. **The Responsible Game move.** *What:* In one drama you have caught, run the move out: notice → name → step out → ask *what is actually happening, what do I actually want, what can I actually choose*. *By when:* Next drama you catch. *Notice:* The Responsible Game feels less stimulating because the gremlin is not being fed. That flatness is not boredom; it is the absence of drama-food. The flat space is where actual choice becomes available.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

52. **One drama refusal per day for a week.** *What:* Each day for seven days, refuse one drama you would normally produce. Skip one complaint conversation. Decline one rescue. Choose not to escalate one mild irritation. *By when:* Seven days. *Notice:* By Day 5 the gremlin gets louder — it is starving and will try harder to find food. That escalation is data, not a setback.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Choose refusals invisible at home: skip one group-chat complaint, decline one office rescue, let one work irritation pass unescalated.

53. **The drama-position rotation map.** *What:* Pick one ongoing relationship. Map your rotation through the three positions in that relationship over the last month. *("With my partner I start in Victim, often shift to Persecutor when they don't rescue me the way I wanted, and end up Rescuing them as compensation for the persecuting.")* *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* The rotation pattern is consistent and predictable. Naming it lets you decline a specific point in the cycle, not just "drama" in general.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Map an out-of-household relationship instead, or do the household one mentally with nothing written down.

## Experiments around LISTENING + SPEAKING (Day 8)

These work the four listenings, the four speakings, and completion loops.

54. **Run a completion loop on one stuck conversation.** *What:* Pick one conversation in your life that has been incomplete — left half-said for weeks, months, or years. Initiate. Speak the unfinished piece cleanly. Ask the other person to reflect back. Confirm or correct. Acknowledge. **Name the conversation as complete, out loud.** *By when:* Within one week. *Notice:* The energy that is released when a long-unfinished conversation is closed. Most learners report it is far larger than they expected.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run the loop on a low-stakes, out-of-household conversation (an old colleague, a friend), or write it first as an unsent letter (the retroactive form). If the stuck conversation is with the person your intake Screen 4 answer named, do not run it with them — this substitution is the point.

55. **Practice each of the four listenings in a single day.** *What:* Tomorrow, in four separate conversations, run one of the four listenings consciously: confirming, meaning, literal, possibility. One per conversation. *By when:* One day. *Notice:* Which is hardest to switch out of? Most learners struggle to drop out of confirming listening — it has been their default for so long.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

56. **Refuse one roadblock you usually deploy.** *What:* Pick one of the 12 Roadblocks (advising, reassuring, interrogating, judging, comparing, etc.) you reach for most. For 24 hours, refuse it. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* The empty space where the roadblock used to be. What happens in the conversation when you do not deploy it? Often the other person speaks differently because they have been given room.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

57. **Hold a vacuum listening space for 5 minutes.** *What:* In one conversation, hold a vacuum listening space for 5 minutes. No nodding, no smiling, no "yes," no question, no comment. Present. Empty. *By when:* Next medium-stakes conversation. *Notice:* The pull to fill the space. The Box's hunger to comment. What did the speaker do in the unfilled space? Most learners report the speaker said things they had never said before.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Run it in a work meeting or with a friend, where five quiet minutes read as attentiveness.

58. **The speaking-from-where audit.** *What:* For one day, three times, mid-sentence, silently ask: *"From which location am I speaking right now?"* — story, sincerity, possibility, or sourcing. *By when:* One day. *Notice:* Most adult speech comes from story. The other three are available, but only when chosen consciously.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

59. **The one-true-sentence experiment.** *What:* In one conversation today, say one true sentence — sourced from your body, not your script. Short. Direct. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* What did your body do before saying it? What did the other person do after? Most learners discover one true sentence shifts a conversation more than an hour of careful explanation.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Say the sentence in an out-of-household conversation first.

60. **The reflection request.** *What:* In one conversation today, after you say something that matters to you, ask: *"Can I check what landed for you?"* Listen to what they say without correcting. *By when:* 24 hours. *Notice:* The gap between what you said and what they heard. The gap is the reason completion loops exist.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Ask it in a work conversation, where it reads as ordinary diligence.

61. **The 12 Roadblocks X-ray.** *What:* For three days, after each conversation that mattered to you, name which of the 12 Roadblocks you deployed instead of listening. *By when:* Three days. *Notice:* The roadblock-of-first-resort is consistent. Yours might be advice, or reassurance, or interrogation, or interpretation. Knowing yours is half the work of declining it.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

## Experiments around EGO STATES + PROBLEM OWNERSHIP (Day 9)

These work the five ego states (Parent, Child, Adult, Gremlin, Demon) and the I/you-statement test for problem ownership.

62. **Name your ego state mid-sentence.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, catch yourself once in the middle of a sentence — out loud, in conversation — and silently ask: *"Which ego state am I speaking from right now?"* Notice. Do not change. Do not announce. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* You will almost certainly catch a state you did not expect to be in. Often Critical Parent when you thought you were in Adult.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

63. **Give back one misowned problem.** *What:* Identify one problem you have been carrying that is not actually yours — someone else's life decision, someone else's emotion, someone else's responsibility you took on. Give it back. The most common form: stop fixing, stop researching, stop strategizing on their behalf. Tell them, if appropriate: *"I am stepping out of working on this. It is yours."* *By when:* Within one week. *Notice:* The space that opens when a misowned problem is set down. Often the other person picks it up faster than you expected.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use the silent form: stop the fixing without the announcement. The sentence is optional everywhere; with a household member it can stay unsaid.

64. **Ask: from which ego state am I about to speak?** *What:* Before three discrete statements tomorrow that matter — in a meeting, in a partner conversation, in a tense exchange — pause for 5 seconds and silently ask the question. Notice. Speak. *By when:* Tomorrow. *Notice:* How often is the answer not Adult? What changes when you let the moment pass before speaking from a non-Adult state?
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

65. **The I-statement / you-statement audit.** *What:* For one day, listen to your own speech. Each time you say "you," check: is the problem you are speaking about *theirs* (correctly assigned) or *yours* (misowned)? *By when:* One day. *Notice:* Most "you-statements" in adult conflict are misowned problems. *"You make me feel…"* is a mis-ownership; the feeling is yours. The owned version is *"I feel… when…"*
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

66. **Catch the Gremlin once a day.** *What:* For three days, catch your Gremlin speaking once per day. Tell — the Gremlin uses a sotto voce voice, often a sentence that "feels true" but in your stomach knows it isn't. *("You're not actually going to do this." "They never appreciate you." "Just one more.")* *By when:* Three days. *Notice:* The Gremlin's vocabulary is repetitive. By Day 3 you can predict its lines. Knowing them in advance is part of declining them.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

67. **The Parent voice attribution.** *What:* Pick one Critical Parent sentence the Box reliably says to you (*"You should know better." "Don't make a fool of yourself." "Good people don't…")*. Identify whose voice it actually is. *By when:* One sitting this week. *Notice:* The voice is almost never yours originally. Naming the original source — your mother, a teacher, a coach — separates you from the voice. The voice continues; your relationship to it changes.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Do it mentally, or in locked notes; the attribution needs no paper trail.

68. **The Free Child invitation.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, do one thing from Free Child — spontaneous, curious, alive, without justifying to your Adult or Critical Parent. Dance in your kitchen. Buy something foolish. Tell a joke that might not land. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* How quickly does the Adapted Child or Critical Parent show up to manage the Free Child? Each interruption is data about which state your daily life is currently being run from.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Take the Free Child out of the house: the playground detour on a walk, singing in the car, the foolish purchase that stays in your bag.

69. **Demon early-warning signs and support conditions.** *What:* In one private sitting, name only the **early warning signs** that your Demon is stirring (a specific physical sensation, a specific kind of intrusive thought, a specific situation that activates it) and the **support conditions** you need when it stirs (who you call, what you do, what you do not do). Deeper Demon work — naming the specific harm-capacity, doing Demon process work — belongs with a trained PM facilitator or trauma-qualified clinician (see Day 9 referral pathway and the GT Gremlin Transformation training). This experiment is the locator, not the process. *By when:* One sitting this week, with your partner reachable and the CM contact at hand. *Notice:* Knowing the early-warning signs lets you reach for support before the Demon is driving. Naming them is the work; processing them is a different room.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Hold the list unwritten (speak it once, alone, in the car), or keep it in locked notes in shorthand only you can read.

## Experiments around POSSIBILITY + THREE POWERS (Day 10 + post-course)

These work the Map of Possibility, bright principles, and the three powers — Choose, Ask Dangerous Questions, Declare.

70. **Ask one dangerous question.** *What:* Ask yourself one dangerous question this week and sit with the answer for at least 20 minutes. No journaling. No solving. Just sit. *("What am I pretending not to know?" "Who would I be if I dropped this story about myself?" "What would I have to be willing to feel in order to leave this?")* *By when:* This week. *Notice:* The dangerous question dissolves the floor; what surfaces in the 20 minutes is what was underneath. Do not act on it within the hour. Let it work.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

71. **Run a 30-day declaration.** *What:* Make one declaration about something specific you commit to for the next 30 days. *("I am running the daily box catch for 30 days." "I am asking one dangerous question a week." "I am the one who is creating an income from my creative work.")* Write the date. Tell your partner. *By when:* This week. *Notice:* The first three days are easy. Day 7–10 the Box starts producing reasons to drop the declaration. That moment is the test. Each rep past Day 10 changes who you are.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Tell your course partner by message from outside the home; keep the written declaration in locked notes.

72. **Choose one bright principle to source from for a week.** *What:* From the bright principles list, pick one (possibility, courage, integrity, joy, intimacy, service, etc.). For one week, in any moment of decision, source from that principle. *("I am sourcing from courage this week.")* *By when:* One week. *Notice:* Sourcing from a principle is different from "trying to embody it." Sourcing means: in a decision moment, ask what action this principle would take here. Then take that action.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

73. **The shadow-principle catch.** *What:* In the next 48 hours, catch yourself sourcing from a shadow principle — fear, control, contempt, righteousness, comfort. Name which. Do not change anything. *By when:* 48 hours. *Notice:* Shadow principles are not "bad"; they are unconscious sourcing. Once conscious, the choice opens.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

74. **The dangerous question for one other person.** *What:* In one conversation this week where the other person is in a held container with you, ask them one dangerous question. Hold the space for their answer. Do not paraphrase. Do not advise. *("What would you have to give up to have what you want?")* *By when:* This week, with one person, with consent. *Notice:* Dangerous questions asked of others without liquid state, without consent, are violence. Asked inside the right container, they are one of the most respectful things you can offer.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Ask it of your course partner inside the voice exchange, not of anyone you live with.

75. **The Power-of-Choosing practice.** *What:* In one moment today where you reacted on autopilot, after the fact, declare: *"I am the one who chose this."* Even though the choice was made unconsciously. Especially though. *By when:* Today. *Notice:* The Power of Choosing is the radically responsible move of owning the unconscious choice as a chosen choice. From there, the second move can be chosen on purpose.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Declare it silently; the move is the owning, not the volume.

76. **The declaration test.** *What:* Test a possible declaration before committing. Out loud, say it. Notice whether your body recognizes it as true or whether the room feels off. *("I am writing the book." "I am leaving this marriage." "I am starting the company.")* *By when:* This week. *Notice:* True declarations land in the body with a kind of weight. Off ones leave a fizz. The body knows.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Test it out loud in the car or on a walk, out of household earshot.

77. **One bright principle, named in one conversation.** *What:* In one conversation today, name the bright principle you are sourcing from out loud — to yourself, or to the other person. *("I want to source from possibility in this conversation, not from fear.")* *By when:* Today. *Notice:* Naming the principle in real time changes the speech that comes out. You cannot speak the same way after naming the principle as before.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Use the to-yourself form only.

## The post-course experiment menu — 90-day commitments

After Day 10, the course closes. The thoughtware embeds through the next 90 days. Pick **one** of these (not all). Commit. Mark the date.

78. **The 90-day Box catch.** *What:* Every day for 90 days, catch your Box once. Write one sentence in your Beep! Book: *Box ran [the specific move] in [the specific moment].* No more, no less. *By when:* 90 days from today. *Notice:* By Day 60 the catching has become automatic. The thoughtware is installing itself.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Locked phone notes in flat shorthand; move to paper later if the book cannot live safely at home.

79. **The 90-day partner exchange.** *What:* Continue voice-message exchanges with your pairing partner once a week for 90 days. Same structure as the course: speak from your body, reply with what landed, ask one open question. *By when:* 90 days. *Notice:* The container persists past the course. The relationship deepens. The reps continue.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Record your messages away from home (the car, a walk); switch to text in weeks when privacy is thin.

80. **The weekly dangerous question.** *What:* Once a week for 13 weeks, ask yourself one dangerous question and sit with the answer for at least 20 minutes. Do not solve. Do not journal until the next day. *By when:* 13 weeks. *Notice:* The dangerous questions get better with practice. Your inventory of which questions land grows.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

81. **The 90-day declaration.** *What:* One declaration. Specific. Public to one person at minimum. Reviewed weekly with the same person. Reinforced every Monday with one sentence: *"I am still running the declaration. Here is what I am noticing."* *By when:* 90 days. *Notice:* The declaration is the most generative practice past Day 10. What gets declared and reinforced becomes who you are.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Make your course partner or an out-of-household friend the one person; review by message.

82. **The monthly module re-read.** *What:* Once a month for three months, re-read one module that landed hardest for you in the course. Note what is now visible that was not the first time. *By when:* Three months. *Notice:* The course's distinctions deepen on re-reading. The first read was the install. The re-reads are when the install starts running.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* — (already private)

83. **The 30 / 60 / 90-day check-in (the container for everything above).** *What:* Before you close Day 10, put **three dates** in your calendar — 30, 60, and 90 days out. At each, spend 15 minutes on four questions: *(1) Am I still running the practice(s) I committed to? (2) What has compounded — what is now easier or automatic that wasn't? (3) What did the Box quietly reabsorb, and when did it slip? (4) What is the one adjustment for the next 30 days?* Optionally voice-message a standing partner the same four answers. *By when:* the three dated check-ins. *Notice:* the check-in is what turns a 90-day "intention" into a 90-day practice. Whichever experiment(s) above you pick, this is the scaffold that keeps them alive — a solo 90-day practice with no check-in is the single most common way the post-course arc dies. Pick this one *plus* one other.
   *Lower-stakes variant:* Label the three calendar entries neutrally ("review"); the 15 minutes is silent and the four answers can live in locked notes.

## Final note

This file contains 75+ experiments. Most learners run 8–12 of them across the 30 days of the course, and 4–6 more in the 90 days after.

Three rules:

1. **One experiment at a time.** Two in parallel — neither runs cleanly.
2. **Capture in three sentences.** Within ten minutes of running. Most learning is lost in the first hour.
3. **The experiment that produces *"I would rather not"* is the one to run.** The Box's resistance is the signal you are at the right experiment. Comfortable experiments produce no new data.
